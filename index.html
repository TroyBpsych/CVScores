Education Quality Report · 2024–25

Central Valley Schools:
A Detailed Cross-Regional Analysis

Overview

California's Central Valley — encompassing counties from Sacramento south through Kern — serves over 1.2 million K–12 students across dozens of school districts. As a region, its schools face some of the most challenging socioeconomic conditions in the state, and this is directly reflected in academic outcomes. Most Central Valley districts score meaningfully below the state average on California's standardized assessments (CAASPP), though there are important exceptions and meaningful pockets of excellence.

The gap between the Valley and the state's best-performing regions — the Bay Area and coastal San Diego — is substantial, often spanning 30 to 60 percentage points on proficiency measures. However, the picture is not monolithic: districts like Clovis Unified consistently outperform the state average and rank among the better-performing large districts in California.

The Central Valley's educational challenges are deeply intertwined with economic and demographic conditions that are among the most difficult in the state. Poverty rates, high proportions of English Learners, large numbers of migrant agricultural workers' children, and lower levels of parental education all correlate with lower academic performance. These factors are not excuses — they are the structural context that districts must navigate, often with less funding per pupil than wealthier coastal areas receive.

Key Findings & Analysis

What the Data Shows

  • Central Valley schools as a region consistently score below the state average in both ELA and math. Proficiency rates in the southern San Joaquin Valley (Kern, Kings, Merced, Tulare) are the lowest in the region, often 10–20 percentage points below even the Central Valley average.
  • The Bay Area and coastal San Diego represent the state's highest-performing zones, with top districts scoring 2–3 times higher than the lowest-performing Central Valley districts on CAASPP.
  • Clovis Unified is the clear standout in the Central Valley — ranking among the top 65 school districts in California and achieving proficiency rates well above the state average, despite serving a mid-sized city. Its individual schools (Clovis North, Buchanan) rank in the top 2–5% statewide.
  • The "two Fresnos" problem: Clovis Unified and Fresno Unified share adjacent geography but have vastly different outcomes. Clovis is largely suburban and wealthier; Fresno Unified serves a much higher concentration of low-income and English Learner students.
  • Sacramento, while technically Central Valley, performs significantly better than the San Joaquin Valley cities to its south, reflecting a more diversified economy and higher average household incomes.
  • Chronic absenteeism is a cross-regional problem but hits the Central Valley particularly hard, exacerbated by poverty, agricultural labor patterns, and pandemic aftereffects.
  • Graduation rates have improved across the Valley — Fresno Unified reached 89%, up from 84% five years ago — showing that outcomes can improve even in challenging environments with sustained investment.
  • Teacher shortages disproportionately affect the Valley, compounded by high cost of housing relative to teacher salaries, lower housing stock in smaller cities, and competition from better-paying suburban districts.

Trends & Outlook

Despite the persistent gap, there are genuine reasons for cautious optimism in the Central Valley. CAASPP scores across California have shown steady year-over-year improvement since pandemic lows, and Central Valley districts have generally improved at similar or slightly above-average rates. Fresno Unified, for instance, saw a 2+ percentage point gain in both ELA and math in 2024–25, and its first-grade literacy proficiency target of 80% by 2030 (up from 48% in 2024) indicates serious institutional ambition.

The region's high birth rates and growing enrollment — in contrast to declining enrollment in coastal districts — mean the Valley will have an increasingly large share of California's student population going forward. How well these students are educated will matter enormously for the state's economic future, particularly given that the Valley will provide a significant share of California's workforce in the coming decades.

Investments in community schools, Universal Transitional Kindergarten, reading coaches, and the state's Local Control Funding Formula (which directs more money to high-need districts) are all targeted partly at reducing the Valley's educational disadvantage. Whether these investments are sufficient to close the gap with the Bay Area and coastal California remains an open question — but the structural challenges are formidable and will require sustained commitment over multiple decades.
